# Product

## Register

product

## Users

PMW employees use the public career portal to browse internal opportunities, review role details, submit applications, and track their own submissions. HR administrators use the authenticated career admin screens to manage openings, review applications, update statuses, and maintain the portal cards. The employee applicant journey is the first priority; HR admin workflows are second and should stay efficient, dense, and consistent.

## Product Purpose

PMW HR Forms centralizes HR form submissions, approvals, evaluations, and internal career workflows. For the career surface, success means employees can quickly understand available opportunities, feel confident submitting an application, and receive clear confirmation. For admins, success means role and application management feels reliable, scannable, and aligned with the rest of the product without slowing down repeated work.

## Brand Personality

Clear, grounded, and branded. The interface should use the company logo palette as its anchor: PMW blue for primary actions, links, focus states, and active navigation; PMW purple for secondary accents and admin/category cues; soft sky and white surfaces for readability. Yellow may appear as a rare attention accent, not as the main brand signal.

## Anti-references

Avoid generic SaaS dashboard styling, loud campaign-poster treatment, over-decorated cards, gray-on-gray admin tables, mixed accent colors without hierarchy, and decorative motion that does not communicate state. The product should not feel like a marketing landing page where users need a work tool.

## Design Principles

1. Employee journey first: optimize the career portal, job detail, application form, and submitted state before admin polish.
2. Stable behavior, clearer flow: preserve routes, SharePoint/API behavior, duplicate checks, PDPA consent, and admin override logic while improving page organization and calls to action.
3. Logo-led identity: use PMW blue and purple as the consistent interaction language across public and admin surfaces.
4. Earned familiarity: keep standard MUI affordances, predictable tables, recognizable controls, and clear status feedback.
5. User-friendly signals: use MUI icons on buttons and state indicators where they improve recognition, with accessible labels and tooltips for icon-only actions.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Use WCAG AA as the practical baseline. Body text and placeholders need sufficient contrast, clickable cards and icon controls need visible keyboard focus, status must not rely on color alone, transitions must respect reduced-motion preferences, and mobile controls should maintain at least a 40px hit area without text crowding.
